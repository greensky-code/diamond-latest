Introduction

The line of business record can be used to categorize different products offered by a health plan. Or, because of the variety of attributes that can be associated with a line of business code, keyword LINBS can distinguish different kinds of products according to how providers, payees, claims, and members are handled.

This document explains the options you have for governing processing depending on the line of business.

How LINBS Affects Claim Pricing

Allowed Amount Versus Billed Amount Edits

While in institutional claims the allowed amount can at times be greater than the billed amount (per diem is one example), this is not generally allowed for professional services or dental services claims. If a line of business does allow this for professional services or dental services claims, you may permit it with an entry in the Allowed > Billed field.

If you select this check box, when professional services or dental services claims associated with this line of business are priced, DIAMOND processing determines the allowed amount and does not lower the allowed amount to equal the billed amount if billed is less than allowed.

See BENEF, Apply Pat Liability To, PSCLM, Add Back Amt, or DNCLM, Add Back Amt User Documentation for additional information.

How LINBS Affects Authorization/Claim Matching

The line of business record determines whether or not DIAMOND processing attempts to automatically match authorizations to claims during claims processing. In other words, the line of business record enables this feature. However, there are many parameters and rules required to enable authorization/claims matching to work. See Authorization Claim Links, How DIAMOND Thinks, which includes references to the other keywords and functions necessary to enable this functionality.

There are several entries in keyword LINBS that, in combination, enable and govern some aspects authorizations/claims matching. They are Auth/Claim Match, Days Before, Days After, Covering Prov, and Waive Match Order. For specific entries and definitions, see LINBS Field Definitions.

The Auth/Claim Match field has three functions.

  1. It enables the authorization/claims matching feature, both for the initial claims processing and the Authorization Match/Waive command on the menu bar in keywords DNCLM, INCLM, and PSCLM.

  2. It turns the same feature for covering providers, if you want to have DIAMOND processing match authorizations to services rendered by covering providers on behalf of the original authorized provider. To enable this feature, both this field and the Covering Prov field must be selected.

  3. It enables you to use the ‘days before’ and ‘days after’ feature used by keyword ARULE, the authorization/claims matching rules.

The Days After and Days Before fields have two functions.

  1. They define the acceptable date range outside of which a claim is considered "outside the authorized date," leading to the claim action specified in keyword ARULE.

  2. They define the acceptable date range in the same way for covering providers.

In both cases and for both Days After and Days Before, the number of days entered is attached to the primary date of service on the claim (PSCLM, the Svc Date on the header window; INCLM, the Admit Date on the header window). If the request date on the authorization is within the calculated range, it is a potentially valid authorization for the subject claim and is eligible for matching.

Important Note  The VENDORFIRST parameter logic cannot be used if you select the Covering Prov check box (the VENDORFIRST Parameter 1 must be "blank.")

Subscriber/Dependent Eligibility Consistency

You can relax the standard edits DIAMOND processing uses to make sure that dependents cannot have eligibility and benefits which are outside of or different from the subscribers. In most commercial health plans, this consistency is critical. In some situations and programs (e.g. Medicaid, often) the eligibility of dependents can be different from that of the head of household or subscriber. Therefore, by line of business you may choose to eliminate this editing.

When consistency tests are enforced, DIAMOND processing requires that all dependents must be enrolled in the same group and plan as the subscriber. In addition, the dependents’ effective and termination dates must fall within the eligibility period of the subscriber; i.e., dependents are eligible for coverage based on the subscriber’s eligibility dates and the dependents will not be eligible after the subscriber is terminated.

If you choose to relax the edits, consistency tests are not enforced. In addition, be aware that:

  • Keyword GPBIL (Premium Billing) cannot be used for this line of business, since billing logic assumes that all members of a family are enrolled in the same benefit plan and that the eligibility periods of the dependents always fall within the eligibility period of the subscriber.

  • Family unit-oriented benefit rules (such a family deductibles) cannot be used for this line of business.

  • The Member Address Ripple command on the Special menu in keyword MEMBR cannot be used for this line of business.

EOBs for Ineligible Members

You may enter a rule in keyword LINBS to print EOBs. An exception to this rule for claims processed online is when a member is determined to be ineligible. When this happens, DIAMOND processing uses the DFLTPRINTSTAT parameters to decide whether to print an EOB.

Default Provider Logic

Keyword PROVC (Provider Contracts) maintains the data that defines the contractual relationship between the healthcare organization and the healthcare provider. During claims processing, DIAMOND processing must determine a relationship between the rendering provider and the member. To do this, DIAMOND processing uses provider contract search logic and the associated line of business contract search criteria sets to "match" a provider’s contract to the member.

Instead of creating and managing unique contract records for each provider, line of business, IPA, and panel combination, it may be more efficient to create and manage only the very specific relationships of a provider to line(s) of business, IPA(s), and panel(s), using more general contracts shared by many providers who have the same relationship with the healthcare organization. This approach can significantly reduce the number of contract records you must enter and maintain.

For more information, see Provider Contracts, How DIAMOND Thinks.
