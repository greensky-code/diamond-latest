How DIAMOND Applies the Correct Calculations

Authorization/Claim Detail Linking and Pricing for Whole Claim Pricing

Institutional claims are often priced in entirety rather than line-by-line. In DIAMOND this is called whole claim pricing. The following components result in whole claim pricing.

1. Pricing records are established

  • A PRULE record which links Detail, Selection, Determinant and Whole Claim records together and ties to the institution’s contract

  • Corresponding Price Rule Details (PRULD)

  • As necessary for PRULD records, corresponding Price Rule Detail Selection (PRULS) records

  • Price Rule Determinants (PDTER)

  • Procedure Code (PROCD) and Procedure Price (PROCP) records for the system generated price line

  • Whole Claim Price Rule (PRULW) records for the determinants established in PDTER

2. The price rule, from PRULE, is entered on the institution contract

While the PRULE record could alternatively be entered on the Group Detail window (GRUPD — Price Rule 1) the most common set up is a unique PRULE record for each institutional provider because institutional contracts tend to vary widely from hospital to hospital.

3. The claims processor enters each submitted claim detail line and invokes whole claim pricing

DIAMOND marks the entered detail lines as informational only (unless they represent services which have been ‘carved out’ of the whole claim pricing logic, in which case they are priced individually), and one or more detail lines with pricing information for the whole claim are automatically generated.

The Price Rule Determinant Table (PDTER)

Many institutional provider contracts require different pricing for different types of claims. For example, some admissions may be reimbursed per diem; others may be paid under a case rate. PDTER is used to identify different claim types, and PRULW records are keyed to those claim types.

Summary of PRULW functions

Within PRULW there are three rule types:

  • Base Rules calculate the primary allowed amount for the claim.

  • Outlier Rules calculate the amount payable (in addition to the primary allowed amount) when a claim exceeds a pre-defined threshold.

  • Cap Rules calculate the difference between the claim amount and the cap limit when the claim amount is higher than the cap limit. The difference is then added as a negative amount in an adjustment line to bring the total for the claim back down to the cap limit.

When you invoke whole claim pricing during claims processing, DIAMOND:

  • Evaluates all applicable whole claim price rule (PRULW) Base, Outlier, and/or Cap rule type records.

  • Generates an additional claim detail line(s) that includes the whole claim priced allowed amount, reason code and procedure code based on the applicable base rule type record(s).

  • Generates additional claim detail lines if any Outlier or Cap rule type records are applicable.

  • Flags automatically generated claim detail lines as "payable" (INCLM, Claim Detail — Claim Status = P), and entered claim detail lines as "informational (Status = I).

How price rule details (PRULD and PRULS) affect whole claim pricing

Even under whole claim pricing, there may be certain services that must be handled on a line-by-line basis.

The most common use of PRULD and PRULS in whole claim pricing is to define three pricing levels, although you may use more than three. Level 1 defines line items, which are reimbursed in addition to the whole claim price, typically called ‘carve-outs’. These carve-outs are excluded from totals which affect the final whole claim-calculated price, such as a whole claim price at a percentage of billed; those carved-out lines would not be included in the billed against which the percentage is applied. Level 2 is used to define items, which are denied, such as patient convenience items. Level 3 defines all line items, other than those defined by Levels 1 and 2, as informational only. These informational lines are included in the total for purposes of reimbursement such as the percentage of the total billed.

PRULD contains the calculation method; PRULS contains ranges of procedure or UB revenue codes (any medical service code that can be entered on a claim detail line), which identify the claim lines to which the calculation method is applied. DIAMOND reads PRULS records using fall-through logic; thus, the most specific PRULS records appear first in sequence, followed by increasingly general rules. Often the last in a sequence of PRULD records needs no PRULS record because it applies to ‘all else.’

How DIAMOND evaluates price rules

In line-by-line pricing, DIAMOND selects the first price rule that matches the claim attributes, applies that rule, and goes on to the next claim line. In other words, DIAMOND stops as soon as it finds a match. Whole claim pricing works differently because multiple price rules can apply to a single claim. Therefore DIAMOND must employ a different kind of logic to know when to stop evaluating rules and attempting to apply them.

Mutually exclusive calculation methods

Some calculation methods are mutually exclusive. In DIAMOND, these are Calc Methods Per Diem, Tiered Per Diem, Whole Claim Percentage of Billed, and DRG. For example, if a claim is being paid according to a per diem, it is not also going to be paid by DRG.

Therefore, every Base Price Rule within each of these Base Price Rule Calc Methods is evaluated, and as soon as any (or all) of the Base Price Rules within one of these groups is applied to the claim, DIAMOND stops evaluating calculation methods.

Case rates

DIAMOND can apply a maximum of three Base Price Rule types with a Calc Method of Case Rt. Case 1 (lowest Seq. No.) is applied at 100%. Case 2 (next highest Seq. No.) and Case 3 (highest Seq. No.) are applied at the percentages defined in the provider’s contract (keyword PROVC, the DRG/APC Information command on the Special menu).

Selecting between two pricing calculations using buckets

Whole claims pricing provides the flexibility to calculate the allowed amount for the claim using two different pricing methods (for example, Per Diem and Percent of Billed) and to decide which of the two calculated allowed amounts to apply, the greater or the lesser amount depending on the entry in PRULD. In DIAMOND this pricing technique is called "Bucket Logic."

Bucket calculations apply all related bucket entries until no more bucket records exist. Multiple entries are used to store the interim calculations, which lead to the final reimbursement amount. For example, if you have a contract that pays DRGs as long as the DRG amount is not less than 60% of the billed amount, you must establish multiple records that calculate the DRG, 60% of the billed amount, compare the two, and select the greater.

Bucket logic can be used to override what would otherwise be mutually exclusive methods.

Sum of Allowed Calculation Method

Sum of Allowed is a whole claim pricing calculation method that allows you to price informational detail lines using DIAMOND’s line-by-line pricing.

 

Sum of Allowed method (calculation method 23) sums all of the "allowed" amounts based on the combination of include/exclude filters you set up in PRULD (Price Rule Detail) and summing details option you set in PRULW (Whole Claim Price Rules). DIAMOND sums all of the allowed amounts for the selected lines, then multiplies that summed amount by the percentage you specified in the Allowed Factor field of the PRULW window.

 

Outlier and cap logic apply.

Setup Considerations for Sum of Allowed Calculation Method

To successfully use Sum of Allowed calculation method:

  • Set up a pricing rule in PRULW using method 23, Sum of Allowed.

  • Place a check in the Informational Only flag on PRULD.

  • Decide if you want to multiply the summed allowed amount by a percentage. DIAMOND uses the percentage you specified in the Allowed Factor field in PRULW to multiply against the summed allowed amount. The default Allowed Factor is 100%.

  • Set the Incl/Exclude fields on PRULD and the Summing Details field on PRULW so that the appropriate claim lines are included. DIAMOND looks at the combination of Incl/Exclude and Summing Details fields to determine which claim lines to include when it calculates the summed allowed amount.

Using Sum of Allowed as Fall-Through Logic

DIAMOND searches through price rules according to the sequence number, starting with the lowest number, and uses the first price rule that matches. Therefore, if you want to set up fall-through logic using a Sum of Allowed Amount rule as the fall-through, then you must make sure that the sequence number you assign to the Sum of Allowed price rule is higher than the sequence number you use for other rules.

Unclean Days on Informational Claim Detail Lines

Unclean days are an option that you can use when calculating interest amounts against claim lines. Informational lines can be marked as unclean. When an informational line is marked as unclean, the number of unclean days are tracked and will be transferred to the resulting system generated payable line. For a complete explanation of unclean days and interest calculations, see Claims Discount and Interest Calculations, How DIAMOND Thinks.
